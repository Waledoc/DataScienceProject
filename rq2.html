RQ2 — Seasonal Patterns in Electricity Consumption

How does electricity consumption vary across seasons in Germany, and how does this relate to seasonal weather conditions?

Research Focus

This analysis examines how electricity consumption changes across the seasons in Germany and how these patterns are related to temperature and other seasonal weather conditions.

Key Results

The analysis of the data from 2022 to 2025 shows a clear seasonal pattern in electricity consumption in Germany. Winter has the highest average daily consumption, while summer has the lowest. Spring and autumn lie in between, with autumn above spring.

The average daily electricity consumption is approximately 2.25 million MWh in winter, 2.08 million MWh in autumn, 2.01 million MWh in spring, and 1.91 million MWh in summer.

This means the ranking is Winter > Autumn > Spring > Summer. The difference between winter and summer is substantial, with winter consumption being about 18 % higher than summer consumption.

In the merged dataset, electricity consumption is moderately negatively correlated with temperature. The correlation with maximum temperature is approximately -0.44, and the correlation with minimum temperature is also approximately -0.44.

Interpretation

Electricity consumption in Germany shows a clear seasonal pattern. Consumption is highest in winter and lowest in summer, which closely matches the temperature structure in the weather data.

A plausible explanation is that colder weather increases electricity demand through higher heating-related demand, more lighting demand during darker months, and generally higher seasonal energy use in households and parts of the economy.

By contrast, summer has the lowest demand because heating needs are minimal and daylight hours are longer. In this dataset, wind and precipitation show much weaker relationships with consumption than temperature does, so temperature appears to be the main seasonal weather factor behind the observed pattern.

Summary: Electricity consumption in Germany is strongly seasonal, with winter demand clearly above summer demand, and temperature is the most important weather-related factor behind this pattern.
