RQ7 — Renewable Generation and Electricity Prices

How are electricity prices related to renewable electricity generation levels, and do high renewable generation periods correspond to lower electricity prices in Germany?

Research Focus

This analysis investigates the relationship between electricity prices and renewable electricity generation in Germany, focusing on whether higher wind and solar generation tends to coincide with lower market prices.

Key Results and Interpretation

The results indicate a clear negative relationship between renewable electricity generation and market prices in Germany. Across the full dataset from 2022 to 2025, the overall correlation between total renewable generation and electricity prices is r = -0.47, suggesting that higher renewable output is generally associated with lower market prices.

This relationship is visible in each individual year as well, with yearly correlations ranging from r = -0.62 in 2022 to r = -0.72 in 2025. This shows that the inverse relationship remains consistent over time, even though the strength of the pattern varies slightly across years.

The component analysis also shows that this effect is not equally strong for all renewable sources. Wind onshore has the clearest negative relationship with electricity prices at r = -0.39, followed by wind offshore at r = -0.28 and hydropower at r = -0.23. In contrast, photovoltaic generation is only weakly negatively correlated at r = -0.06, while biomass and other renewables show small positive correlations.

A key feature of the visualizations is the exceptional price peak in 2022. Compared with 2023 to 2025, the 2022 observations show much higher and more volatile electricity prices. This is consistent with the broader European energy crisis, which was strongly influenced by the war in Ukraine and the reduction, and later halt, of Russian gas supplies. Since gas-fired power plants often play an important role in electricity price formation, the gas supply shock translated into unusually high electricity prices in Germany.

Overall, the findings suggest that higher renewable generation tends to reduce electricity prices, but this relationship can be distorted during crisis periods such as 2022, when external market shocks pushed prices to unusually high levels.

Visualization 1 — Renewables versus Price Time Series

This time-series graph shows renewable generation and electricity prices in Germany from 2022 to 2025. The electricity price line reaches its highest levels in 2022, with several pronounced peaks that are clearly above the values in the following years.

Across the full period, higher renewable generation often appears alongside lower electricity prices, while lower generation is more often accompanied by higher prices. After 2022, the price series becomes noticeably lower and more stable.

Visualization 2 — Renewables versus Price Scatter

This scatterplot shows the relationship between renewable generation and electricity prices in Germany from 2022 to 2025. The points follow a clear downward pattern, meaning that higher renewable generation levels are generally associated with lower electricity prices.

The 2022 observations stand out most clearly, with several points reaching much higher price levels than the other years. In contrast, the data points from 2023 to 2025 are more concentrated in a lower price range.

Visualization 3 — Renewable Components and Price Correlation

This bar chart compares the correlation values of different renewable generation components with electricity prices. Wind onshore shows the strongest negative correlation, followed by wind offshore and hydropower.

Photovoltaic generation is close to zero, while biomass and other renewables show small positive correlations. Overall, the chart highlights that the relationship with electricity prices differs across renewable sources.
