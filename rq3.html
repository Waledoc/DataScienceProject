RQ3 — Weather Influence on Renewable Generation

How strongly do weather conditions, especially wind speed and sunshine or solar radiation, explain daily electricity generation from wind and photovoltaic in Germany?

Research Focus

This analysis examines how strongly renewable electricity generation in Germany is related to weather conditions, with a particular focus on wind speed for wind generation and favorable weather conditions for photovoltaic generation.

Key Results

The merged Germany-wide dataset from 2022 to 2025 shows that weather conditions explain renewable electricity generation quite clearly, especially for wind power and photovoltaic generation.

The strongest correlation in the dataset appears between wind onshore generation and wind speed, with a correlation of approximately r = 0.58.

For photovoltaic generation, the strongest relationships appear with temperature. The correlation between PV generation and maximum temperature is approximately r = 0.73, while the correlation with minimum temperature is approximately r = 0.60.

Overall, this suggests a moderate to strong weather influence for wind generation and a strong weather relationship for PV generation in the dataset.

Interpretation

The results indicate that wind power reacts clearly to changing wind conditions, which matches the physical expectations for wind generation.

Photovoltaic generation also shows a strong weather relationship. However, the current weather dataset does not include a direct sunshine or solar radiation variable. Instead, temperature acts as an indirect proxy for favorable solar conditions in the aggregated daily data.

This means wind generation can be directly linked to wind speed, while PV generation can only be linked indirectly to favorable weather conditions in the current dataset because direct solar irradiation data is not included.

Precipitation appears to be much less important than wind speed or temperature in explaining renewable output.

Summary: Weather conditions explain renewable generation in Germany quite clearly, with wind speed being a key factor for wind power and temperature acting as a proxy for favorable photovoltaic conditions.
