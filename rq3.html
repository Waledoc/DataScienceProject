RQ3 — Weather Influence on Renewable Generation

How strongly do weather conditions, especially wind speed and sunshine or solar radiation, explain daily electricity generation from wind and photovoltaic in Germany?

Research Focus

This analysis examines how strongly renewable electricity generation in Germany is related to weather conditions, with a particular focus on wind speed for wind generation and favorable weather conditions for photovoltaic generation.

Key Results

The merged Germany-wide dataset from 2022 to 2025 shows that weather conditions explain renewable electricity generation quite clearly, especially for solar generation and wind onshore generation.

The strongest relationship in the dataset appears between solar generation and sunshine duration, with a correlation of approximately r = 0.92.

For wind onshore generation, the relationship with wind speed is also very strong, with a correlation of approximately r = 0.85.

Overall, this suggests that both solar and wind generation are strongly influenced by weather conditions, with sunshine duration showing the clearest connection for solar output and wind speed showing a strong, but slightly more dispersed, relationship for wind generation.

Visualization 1 — Weather and Generation Dashboard

The time-series graphs show how renewable electricity generation and weather conditions develop over time between 2022 and 2025. For wind, the overall pattern of highs and lows follows wind speed quite clearly, even though the relationship is not perfectly proportional. For solar, sunshine duration aligns more closely with generation than temperature, and the graphs also highlight a clear seasonal cycle: solar dominates in the warmer months, while wind is stronger during autumn and winter.

Visualization 2 — Scatter Dashboard

This scatterplot shows the relationship between renewable electricity generation and the selected weather variable. For solar energy, the points are closely clustered, indicating a very strong positive relationship between sunshine duration and solar generation. For wind onshore, the relationship is also clearly positive, but the data is more dispersed, especially at higher wind speeds, which suggests that wind generation does not increase perfectly proportionally with stronger wind.
