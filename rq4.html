RQ4 — Generation Mix during High Renewable Output

How does Germany’s electricity generation mix change on days with high renewable output, and which conventional sources decrease the most?

Research Focus

This analysis examines how Germany’s electricity generation mix changes on days with high wind and photovoltaic output and which conventional sources are reduced the most.

Key Results

The data suggests that on days with high renewable output, Germany’s electricity generation mix shifts noticeably toward wind and photovoltaic generation, while conventional sources are reduced.

In particular, lignite and hard coal show the clearest decline, while gas also decreases but usually less consistently.

On days with a lot of wind and PV generation, the renewable share of the electricity mix increases strongly, the contribution of conventional generation falls, and the mix becomes less dependent on coal and gas.

This indicates a clear substitution effect: when renewable generation is high, conventional plants are needed less to cover demand.

Interpretation

Lignite is one of the largest conventional generation sources in the dataset and therefore shows the most visible downward movement when wind and photovoltaic generation are especially strong.

Hard coal also tends to lose a noticeable share of the generation mix in high-renewable periods. Gas usually declines as well, but often less consistently because it also plays a role in system flexibility and balancing.

Overall, the results are consistent with the idea that renewable electricity displaces fossil generation. When wind and PV produce a lot of electricity, Germany needs less conventional backup in the mix.

Summary: On days with high wind and PV generation, Germany’s electricity mix becomes much more renewable, while conventional generation declines. Among the conventional sources, lignite and hard coal are reduced the most, with gas also decreasing but often less strongly.
